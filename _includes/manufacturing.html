Core Manufacturing Technologies

With over a decade in life-science product development, I've worked across a wide range of manufacturing processes - from the everyday to the highly specialised. If something unfamiliar comes up, I know how to track down the right expertise and get it made.

CNC Machining

CNC machining removes material from a stock block to produce the required geometry. Most prototypes and many products rely on machined parts, and I’ve designed components across a wide range of materials. Through close collaboration with local machining partners, I have rapid access to modern 5-axis CNC machines and CNC lathes. By reviewing designs directly with programmers, I ensure parts are optimised for their processes, with typical turnaround times of 5–10 working days in common materials.

Sheet Metal

Sheet metal components are produced by cutting flat profiles and folding them into the required geometry. I have extensive experience in sheet metal design and work closely with trusted local suppliers to tackle challenging requirements. This collaboration allows us to deliver parts in as little as 7–10 working days, with a wide range of finishing options including welding, powder coating, anodising and more, available in both low and high volumes.

3D Printing

Modern 3D printing can produce almost any geometry, with an expanding range of materials covering stiffness, toughness, chemical resistance and more. I work closely with local suppliers offering SLS, FDM, SLA and large-format FDM, with parts often available as quickly as next day—making it an excellent route for early prototypes. I also maintain two in-house FDM printers, which I use to de-risk and validate designs during development.

Laser/Water Cutting

Laser cutting provides fast, precise 2D profiling across a wide range of materials. Sheet metal suppliers typically handle their own metal cutting in-house, while trusted local partners support us with non-metallic materials such as plastics and composites. This approach provides rapid access to accurate flat parts, either as standalone components or integrated into larger assemblies, or feed into secondary processes.

Thermoforming

Thermoforming shapes heated plastic sheets over moulds to create lightweight, durable components. This can be useful for packaging, covers, and often early prototypes of moulded parts. Because the process demands significant expertise, our close relationship with an excellent local supplier is a real advantage. By drawing on their experience early in the design stage, we ensure parts are optimised for manufacturability and delivered efficiently, whether as prototypes or production-ready components.

Casting

Casting involves pouring molten material into a mould to form complex, often large-scale parts with excellent strength and durability. It is particularly suited to components that would be difficult or uneconomical to machine, such as housings or other intricate geometries. I first encountered casting during my time at Terex, where I gained a strong grounding in the design rules and constraints of the process. While I don't maintain close supplier contacts in this area, I know where to turn should the requirement arise.

Moulding

Moulding processes—such as injection, vacuum, compression and blow moulding, enable the production of an exceptionally wide variety of parts. Depending on the technique, components can be flexible, rigid, transparent, textured, or produced at high volume and low cost. With strong relationships across the moulding industry, I can draw on different suppliers' expertise to match each design to the right process, ensuring efficient delivery from early prototypes through to full-scale production.
